# From Screen to Fabric: How Our PDF Export Works

The moment of truth for any quilt design tool is when the digital design meets real fabric. If the templates are even slightly off, patches will not fit together. If the seam allowances are wrong, your finished quilt will be the wrong size. This post explains how QuiltCorgi ensures your exports are production-accurate.

## The Accuracy Challenge

Quilting demands precision measured in fractions of an inch. A quarter-inch seam allowance is the universal standard, and even small deviations compound across a full quilt. A 1/16-inch error per patch becomes a half-inch error across eight patches — enough to make blocks that will not fit together.

This means our PDF export system cannot simply scale a screen image to paper. It must compute exact geometric coordinates for every patch at finished size, add mathematically precise seam allowances, and generate a PDF where the printed output matches the real-world dimensions exactly.

## How the Pipeline Works

### Step 1: Geometry Extraction

When you click Export, QuiltCorgi extracts the mathematical definition of every patch in your quilt. This is not a screenshot — it is the actual coordinate geometry from the design engine:

- Patch vertices (corners) at finished size
- Seam line definitions (straight or curved)
- Block placement within the quilt grid
- Border and sashing dimensions

### Step 2: Template Deduplication

A quilt might contain thousands of patches, but many share the same shape. An Ohio Star block has only 3 unique template shapes, even though it has 13 patches. A 4x4 grid of Ohio Stars has 208 patches but still only 3 unique templates.

The deduplication algorithm:

1. Computes a normalized representation of each patch shape (translation, rotation, and mirror-invariant)
2. Groups identical shapes into template classes
3. Counts how many of each template are needed, broken down by color/fabric
4. Assigns template IDs for reference

This dramatically reduces the number of pages in your PDF.

### Step 3: Seam Allowance Computation

Adding seam allowance is more complex than simply making each template bigger. The correct approach is an inward offset of the sew line from the cut line:

- **Straight edges** — Offset by the seam allowance distance (default 1/4 inch)
- **Convex corners** — The cut line extends to the intersection of the two offset edges
- **Concave corners** — Requires a notch or clip in the seam allowance to allow the fabric to lie flat
- **Curves** — The offset follows a parallel curve at the specified distance

QuiltCorgi computes these offsets geometrically for every edge and corner, producing mathematically exact cut lines.

### Step 4: PDF Generation

The final step assembles everything into a multi-page PDF:

**Cover page:**
- Quilt name, dimensions, and block count
- Color/fabric legend with swatch previews
- Total yardage estimates

**Assembly diagram:**
- Full quilt layout at reduced scale
- Block labels matching template references
- Border and sashing annotations

**Template pages:**
- One page per unique template
- Actual-size outline with seam allowance
- Grain line arrow showing recommended fabric orientation
- Template ID and cut count per fabric
- Registration marks for verification

**Cutting instructions:**
- Organized by fabric/color
- Template ID, shape description, and count
- Strip-cutting suggestions where applicable

### Step 5: Verification

Before the PDF is delivered, an automated verification pass checks:

- All templates fit within the selected paper size
- Seam allowances do not create templates that extend beyond page margins
- The total cut count matches the quilt's patch count
- Registration marks are correctly placed

## Printing Tips

Even a perfectly generated PDF can produce inaccurate templates if printed incorrectly:

- **Always use "Actual Size" or "100%"** in your print dialog — never "Fit to Page" or "Shrink to Fit"
- **Verify with the measurement check** — Every template page includes a 1-inch and 5-cm reference line. Measure it with a ruler after printing.
- **Use quality paper** — Standard 20lb copy paper works fine for templates. For foundation paper piecing, use lightweight 15lb paper for easier removal.
- **Consider your printer's margins** — Inkjet printers typically have larger margins than lasers. QuiltCorgi accounts for standard margins but check your printer's specifications.

## Foundation Paper Piecing Templates

FPP export follows additional rules:

- Templates are **mirrored** because you sew on the back of the paper
- Sections are **numbered** in sewing order based on seam line analysis
- **Trim lines** show where to cut after sewing each seam
- **Generous margins** extend beyond the seam allowance to ensure fabric coverage

The sewing order is computed by analyzing the block's seam line topology. QuiltCorgi finds a sequence where each new section can be added with a single straight seam, which is the fundamental requirement of foundation paper piecing.

## Rotary Cutting Charts

For patches that are standard geometric shapes (squares, rectangles, half-square triangles, quarter-square triangles), QuiltCorgi generates rotary cutting charts as an alternative to templates:

- Strip width and cutting instructions
- Sub-cut dimensions for each shape
- Total strip length per fabric
- Waste calculations

Irregular shapes that cannot be rotary cut fall back to template cutting, and the chart clearly indicates which patches require templates.
