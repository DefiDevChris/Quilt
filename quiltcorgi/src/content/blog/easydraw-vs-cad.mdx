# EasyDraw vs Traditional CAD: A New Way to Design Blocks

When we designed QuiltCorgi's block drafting tool, we started with a question: how do quilters actually think about block construction? The answer led us away from traditional CAD approaches and toward something fundamentally different.

## How Traditional CAD Works

Most existing quilt design software uses a CAD (Computer-Aided Design) approach borrowed from engineering and architecture. You draw individual patches — shapes that represent pieces of fabric — and arrange them to fill a block:

1. Select a shape tool (rectangle, triangle, polygon)
2. Draw each patch as a separate object
3. Position patches precisely so edges align
4. Repeat until the block is complete

This works, but it has significant problems:

- **Tedious precision** — Every patch must be sized and positioned exactly, or gaps and overlaps appear
- **No validation** — The software does not know if your patches actually form a sewable block
- **Hard to modify** — Moving one patch often breaks alignment with its neighbors
- **Counter-intuitive** — Quilters think in seam lines, not individual patch shapes

## How EasyDraw Works

EasyDraw inverts the paradigm. Instead of drawing patches, you draw seam lines — the lines where fabric pieces meet when sewn together. QuiltCorgi then automatically determines the patches created by those lines.

1. Draw a line from one edge of the block to another
2. The line divides the block into regions
3. Each region automatically becomes a patch
4. Continue adding lines to subdivide further

This approach is closer to how quilters actually think. When a quilter looks at a block design, they see the seam lines first and the patches second. EasyDraw mirrors that mental model.

## A Concrete Example

Consider designing an Ohio Star block:

### Traditional CAD Approach

1. Draw a large center square
2. Draw four half-square triangle pairs for the star points (8 individual triangles)
3. Draw four corner squares
4. Align all 13 pieces precisely so edges match
5. Verify there are no gaps or overlaps

### EasyDraw Approach

1. Draw two horizontal lines dividing the block into thirds
2. Draw two vertical lines dividing the block into thirds (9 cells created)
3. Draw diagonal lines in the four side cells (star points created)
4. Done — 13 patches identified automatically

The EasyDraw version takes 8 lines instead of 13 separate shape operations. More importantly, every patch is guaranteed to be valid because they are defined by intersecting seam lines rather than manually positioned shapes.

## Why Seam Lines Matter

### Guaranteed Validity

In EasyDraw, every region bounded by seam lines and block edges is automatically a valid patch. There are no gaps between patches because patches are defined by the lines themselves. This eliminates the most common and frustrating error in traditional CAD block design.

### Easy Modification

Moving a seam line node in EasyDraw simultaneously adjusts all patches on both sides of that line. In traditional CAD, you would need to resize and reposition multiple patches independently.

### Natural Curve Support

EasyDraw supports curved seam lines. Drawing a curve automatically creates curved patches on both sides. In traditional CAD, creating curved patches requires drawing complex bezier shapes that align perfectly — an exercise in frustration.

### Foundation Paper Piecing Awareness

Because EasyDraw knows the seam line structure (not just the patch shapes), it can automatically generate foundation paper piecing sequences. It analyzes the order in which seam lines can be sewn and numbers the sections accordingly.

## When Traditional Approaches Still Win

EasyDraw is optimized for pieced blocks — designs where fabric pieces share seam lines. For some design types, other approaches work better:

- **Applique** — Layered shapes that do not share seam lines. QuiltCorgi has a dedicated [Applique tool](/tutorials/applique-designs) for this.
- **Whole-cloth** — Designs that are quilted (stitched) rather than pieced. These are better handled in traditional illustration software.
- **Irregular art quilts** — Freeform compositions without block structure might be more natural in general-purpose design tools.

## The Technical Details

For those curious about how EasyDraw works under the hood:

1. **Line intersection** — When you draw a new seam line, QuiltCorgi computes intersections with all existing lines and the block boundary
2. **Region detection** — A planar subdivision algorithm identifies all closed regions (patches) formed by the line network
3. **Validation** — Each region is checked for area (too small = unsewable), angle constraints (too acute = difficult), and closure
4. **Template grouping** — Patches with identical shapes are grouped into template classes, reducing the number of unique cutting templates

All of this happens in real time as you draw, with instant visual feedback.
