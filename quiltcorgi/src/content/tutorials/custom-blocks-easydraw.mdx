# Drawing Custom Blocks with EasyDraw

EasyDraw is QuiltCorgi's seam-line block drafting tool. Instead of drawing individual patches, you draw the seam lines that divide a block into regions — then QuiltCorgi automatically figures out the patches for you.

## Opening EasyDraw

There are two ways to open the block drafting modal:

1. Switch to the **Block worktable** using the tab control in the top bar, then click **New Block**
2. From the Quilt worktable, click a cell and choose **Edit Block** from the context menu

The Block Drafting Modal opens with three tabs: **Freeform**, **EasyDraw**, and **Applique**. Click the **EasyDraw** tab.

## Understanding the EasyDraw Canvas

The EasyDraw canvas shows a square block area with a subtle grid overlay. Key elements:

- **Block boundary** — The outer square that defines your block edges
- **Grid snapping** — Lines snap to the grid for precision (toggle with `G`)
- **Node handles** — Small circles at line endpoints that you can drag to adjust

> **Tip:** Use `Ctrl+Z` / `Cmd+Z` to undo any line you draw. EasyDraw supports unlimited undo history within the session.

## Step 1: Draw Your First Seam Line

Select the **Line tool** from the EasyDraw toolbar (or press `L`). Click a starting point on the block boundary, then click an ending point. A straight seam line appears between the two points.

Try drawing a diagonal from the top-left corner to the bottom-right corner. You have just divided your block into two triangle patches.

## Step 2: Add More Lines

Continue drawing lines to subdivide the block further. Common starting patterns include:

- **Four-patch** — One horizontal and one vertical line through the center
- **Nine-patch** — Two horizontal and two vertical lines creating a 3x3 grid
- **Diagonal star** — Diagonals from corners plus horizontal and vertical center lines

Lines must start and end on either the block boundary or an existing seam line. This constraint ensures every region forms a valid, sewable patch.

## Step 3: Edit Nodes

Switch to the **Select tool** (press `V`) and click any node handle to select it. You can then:

- **Drag** the node to reposition it along the boundary or seam line
- **Delete** the node (press `Delete`) to remove a line segment
- **Split** a line by double-clicking its midpoint to add a new node

Node editing is where the real creativity happens. Small adjustments to node positions can transform a simple grid into an intricate design.

## Step 4: Use Curves

For organic shapes, switch to the **Curve tool** (press `C`). Click to set control points and draw smooth Bezier curves. Curves work the same as lines — they must connect boundary points or existing seam intersections.

> **Note:** Curved seams are beautiful but harder to sew. QuiltCorgi marks curved patches with a small indicator so you know which templates need special attention during cutting.

## Step 5: Preview Your Block

Click the **Preview** button to see your block with automatic coloring applied. QuiltCorgi assigns colors to each patch so you can evaluate the design. The preview also shows:

- Total number of unique patches
- Number of unique templates (patches that share a shape)
- Whether all regions are valid (closed, sewable shapes)

If any region is not valid, it will be highlighted in red. Go back and adjust your seam lines to close any gaps.

## Step 6: Save to Your Library

When you are satisfied with your block, click **Save Block**. Give it a name and optionally assign a category. The block is saved to your "My Blocks" section in the [Block Library](/tutorials/block-library).

Saved blocks work just like built-in blocks — you can drag them into any project, recolor them, and apply fabrics.

## Tips for Great Block Designs

- Start simple — a few well-placed lines often create more striking designs than many lines
- Use symmetry — try mirroring your design across the center for balanced blocks
- Check sewability — avoid tiny triangles and very acute angles that are difficult to piece
- Name descriptively — future you will thank present you when browsing "My Blocks" later
