# Creating Applique Designs

Applique quilting involves layering fabric shapes on top of a background rather than piecing patches together at seams. QuiltCorgi's Applique tool makes designing these layered compositions intuitive.

## Opening the Applique Tool

Open the Block Drafting Modal (from the Block worktable or by editing a block in your quilt) and switch to the **Applique** tab. The canvas shows a blank background square — this represents your base fabric.

## Understanding Applique vs Piecing

In traditional piecing (like [EasyDraw](/tutorials/custom-blocks-easydraw)), patches share seam lines and fit together like puzzle pieces. Applique is different:

- Shapes are layered on top of a background
- Shapes can overlap each other
- Each shape is a complete, independent outline
- Stitching holds shapes to the background (or to each other)

This gives you creative freedom to place shapes anywhere, at any size, in any arrangement.

## Step 1: Choose a Shape Tool

The Applique toolbar offers several shape tools:

- **Circle / Ellipse** — Click and drag to draw round shapes
- **Rectangle** — Click and drag for squares and rectangles
- **Triangle** — Click to place three points
- **Polygon** — Click multiple points to build any custom outline, double-click to close
- **Freehand** — Draw organic shapes with your mouse or stylus

Select a shape tool and draw your first shape on the canvas.

## Step 2: Position and Resize

After drawing a shape, switch to the **Select tool** (press `V`). Click a shape to select it and you can:

- **Move** — Drag the shape to reposition it on the background
- **Resize** — Drag a corner handle to scale the shape proportionally (hold `Shift` for free scaling)
- **Rotate** — Drag the rotation handle above the shape to spin it to any angle
- **Layer order** — Use the up/down arrows in the context panel (or `Ctrl+]` / `Ctrl+[`) to bring shapes forward or send them backward

## Step 3: Layer Multiple Shapes

Build your design by adding more shapes. The layer stack in the context panel shows all shapes from bottom (background) to top. Common applique compositions include:

- **Floral** — Circles for flowers, elongated ellipses for petals, curved polygons for leaves
- **Pictorial** — Combine basic shapes to build houses, animals, or scenes
- **Abstract** — Overlap geometric shapes for modern art-quilt designs

> **Tip:** Hold `Alt` while dragging a shape to duplicate it. This is handy for creating symmetrical arrangements like flower petals.

## Step 4: Overlap Detection

QuiltCorgi automatically detects where shapes overlap. Overlapping regions are highlighted with a subtle hatch pattern so you can see exactly how layers interact. This is important for:

- Planning your stitching order (bottom shapes go down first)
- Understanding how much fabric is hidden under upper layers
- Deciding on seam allowance for tucked edges

The context panel shows overlap warnings if shapes create complex layering that might be difficult to execute.

## Step 5: Assign Fabrics and Colors

Select any shape and assign a color or fabric from the context panel. Each shape can have:

- A fill color or uploaded fabric image
- An outline color (representing the applique stitch line)
- Opacity control for artistic overlays

For fabric assignment, see the [Fabric Management & Fussy Cutting](/tutorials/fabric-fussy-cutting) tutorial — fussy cutting is especially powerful for applique designs where you want specific motifs centered on each shape.

## Step 6: Export Your Design

Applique blocks export differently from pieced blocks. Click **Save Block** to add it to your library, or export directly:

- **Full-size templates** — Each shape prints at actual size for tracing onto fabric
- **Placement guide** — A full-block printout showing where each shape goes
- **Cutting list** — Materials needed for each shape with seam allowance added

See [Exporting: PDF Templates, FPP & Cutting Charts](/tutorials/exporting) for complete export options.

## Design Tips

- Start with the background and work upward
- Group related shapes (like all petals of a flower) and move them together
- Use the grid overlay for alignment (`G` to toggle)
- Preview often — the 3D-style layered preview helps you visualize the finished look
