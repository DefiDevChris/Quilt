# Photo to Quilt: Turn Any Image into a Pattern

QuiltCorgi's Photo Patchwork tool turns any photograph into a quilt-ready pattern. The tool analyzes your image, maps regions to quilt patches, and generates a design you can sew from real fabric.

## How Photo Patchwork Works

The process involves three stages:

1. **Gridding** — Your photo is divided into a grid of cells
2. **Color reduction** — Each cell's average color is matched to a limited palette
3. **Fabric mapping** — Palette colors are assigned to available fabrics

The result is a pixelated version of your photo, where each pixel is a fabric patch in your quilt.

## Step 1: Upload Your Photo

Switch to the **Image worktable** using the top bar tab control. Click **Import Photo** and select an image. Supported formats include JPG, PNG, and WebP.

Choose photos with:

- Strong contrast between subject and background
- Bold, distinct color areas
- Simple compositions (portraits, landscapes, pets work well)
- Good resolution (at least 800x800 pixels)

> **Tip:** Crop your photo before uploading to focus on the most important part of the image. A tightly cropped portrait of your dog will make a much better quilt than a wide shot of a field with a tiny dog in it.

## Step 2: Set the Grid Size

After uploading, configure the grid:

- **Columns** — How many patches wide (typical range: 30-80)
- **Rows** — Calculated automatically to maintain aspect ratio, or set manually
- **Patch size** — The real-world size of each patch (e.g., 2 inches)

The tradeoff is clear: more patches means more detail but more sewing. A 50x60 grid gives good detail for a throw-size quilt with 3,000 patches. A 30x36 grid is more manageable at 1,080 patches.

The preview updates in real time as you adjust the grid size. Watch the image become more or less detailed as you dial in the right balance.

## Step 3: Reduce the Color Palette

Your photo might contain millions of colors, but your quilt needs a practical number of fabrics. The **Color Palette** control lets you specify how many colors to use.

- **Automatic** — QuiltCorgi analyzes the image and suggests an optimal color count (typically 8-20)
- **Manual** — Set the exact number of colors (fewer = more abstract, more = more realistic)
- **Custom palette** — Provide specific colors that match your fabric stash

### Color Mapping Algorithm

QuiltCorgi uses a perceptual color mapping algorithm that prioritizes visual accuracy over mathematical precision. This means:

- Skin tones are handled with care to avoid unnatural shifts
- Dark and light values are preserved for contrast
- Similar hues are merged intelligently

## Step 4: Map to Fabrics

Once your color palette is set, assign real fabrics to each palette color:

1. Click a color swatch in the palette panel
2. Choose from your uploaded fabric library
3. The quilt preview updates to show the actual fabric in each patch

This is where the design comes alive. A photo quilt rendered in batiks looks completely different from the same pattern in solids or novelty prints.

> **See also:** [Fabric Management & Fussy Cutting](/tutorials/fabric-fussy-cutting) for uploading and calibrating fabric images.

## Step 5: Fine-Tune

After the initial conversion, you can manually adjust individual patches:

- **Paint mode** — Click patches to change their color/fabric assignment
- **Smooth** — Reduce noise by averaging small isolated patches into their surrounding color
- **Sharpen** — Enhance edges between color regions for clearer definition
- **Threshold** — Adjust the brightness cutoff for converting gray areas

## Step 6: Transfer to Quilt Worktable

When satisfied with your photo patchwork design, click **Send to Quilt**. This transfers the design to the Quilt worktable where you can:

- Add borders and sashing
- Apply layout modifications
- Export as PDF templates or cutting charts

## Tips for Great Photo Quilts

- Start with a high-contrast photo and fewer colors for your first attempt
- Hang a test print of the gridded image on a wall and step back — quilts are viewed from a distance
- Consider using a single fabric collection for a cohesive look
- Solid fabrics often read better than prints at small patch sizes
